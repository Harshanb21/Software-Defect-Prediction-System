\begin{abstract}
Software systems are basically the backbone of modern life now. We rely on them for everything—communication, running businesses, and keeping critical infrastructure up and running. But as these systems keep growing and getting more complicated, making sure they’re actually reliable becomes a real headache. Traditional testing is exhausting—it takes a lot of manual work, and honestly, it just doesn’t catch enough defects early on. That’s where Software Defect Prediction (SDP) comes in. The idea is simple: use past data and machine learning to spot which parts of the software are most likely to fail, and do it early, before problems go out the door. In this paper, we introduce a Quantum Enhanced Explainable Context-Aware Software Defect Prediction Framework. That’s a big mouthful, but here’s what it means. We combine standard software metrics with some “quantum-inspired” tricks to transform features, then bring in explainable AI methods so developers aren’t left wondering why the model made a certain call. Our approach relies on Quantum Feature Fusion (QFF). This fuses software metrics and converts them into a frequency space—a fancy way to say it looks at the rhythm and patterns in the data, not just the raw numbers. Once those features are ready, we feed them into machine learning models like SVM, Random Forest, and XGBoost. As for explanations, we use SHAP to break down which features matter most for any given prediction. We tested all of this on NASA’s MDP software defect datasets. The results were clear: the quantum-inspired feature transformation and explainable AI combo boosted both accuracy and interpretability. In short, this framework helps developers catch defects early, and more importantly, understand the “why” behind predictions.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Today’s software runs everything from chat apps to bank operations—no exaggeration. But as software gets more complex, keeping it bug-free gets tougher. Testing takes forever, and most teams don’t have unlimited time, money, or people.

In the SDLC, developers and QA folks loop through finding and fixing defects, but with huge systems, it’s not realistic to check absolutely everything. So, teams need smarter tools—something that can predict where the problems might be before it’s too late.

Software Defect Prediction works by training models on old project data and learning to spot modules likely to have issues. This saves time, money, and frustration. Classic algorithms like decision trees, SVM, and boosting are popular, but they tend to stick to basic, static metrics. That’s a problem—these models often miss important context, like how much code is being changed, who’s committing, and when. Even worse, they usually spit out predictions without any real explanation.

Recent studies point out two big gaps: not enough attention to context-aware features, and poor transparency in predictions. So, accuracy suffers, and developers are left guessing. To solve this, our work introduces a Quantum-Enhanced Explainable Context-Aware Framework. We mix static and change-related features, use quantum-inspired feature fusion, then apply XGBoost (a top-classifier), and top it off with SHAP for explainability. The experiments show better accuracy, clearer explanations, and a design that works both in lab research and in real-life software teams.

\subsection{Motivation of the Study}
The reason for jumping into this research is pretty clear—software just keeps getting bigger and more complicated. Companies want updates fast, bugs fixed even faster, and they don’t want to spend a fortune doing it. Old-school “test everything” approaches just don’t scale. That’s why we need smarter ways to catch defects sooner rather than later.

Most current defect prediction models still stick to basic static metrics and machine learning. Sure, they work okay, but they miss important signals—like code churn, commit patterns, and what developers are actually doing. Because of that, old models don’t keep up in rapidly changing projects.

Another problem? Many advanced models act as black boxes. They might be accurate, but developers can’t see why or how decisions get made. That’s a major trust issue. Transparent, explainable predictions are absolutely necessary for real-world adoption.

Quantum-inspired feature engineering offers a new way to look at relationships in the data—in theory, helping models find the subtle patterns regular approaches miss. But it hasn’t really been used for software defect prediction yet. In this study, we bring together contextual data, quantum-enhanced representation, and SHAP explainability, building a framework that’s accurate, open, and practical.

\section{Literature Survey}

Software Defect Prediction (SDP) has attracted significant attention in software engineering because of its ability to optimize testing efforts and enhance software quality. In Menzies et al. (2007), the authors use machine learning approaches like Naïve Bayes and decision trees for predicting defects based on software code metrics. In the article, the researchers used datasets from NASA MDP, which only includes static features. In a similar study, Lessmann et al. (2008) present a comparative benchmarking study of multiple classifiers: Support Vector Machines (SVM), Random Forest, Logistic Regression, among others. This research proves the importance of proper model selection in SDP. Further developments in SDP involve the investigation of software evolution history as done by Hall et al. (2012) and Nam et al. (2015). Nam et al. introduce the concept of transfer learning in SDP, which allows transferring models between projects by retraining on similar data. As such, SDP using transfer learning greatly depends on datasets similarity. Deep learning techniques became popular in SDP research, with Chen et al. (2016) and Wang et al. (2016) using neural networks and convolution models for prediction. Other research on SDP uses ensemble learning to create accurate models (Li et al., 2017 and Panichella et al., 2018). In a systematic comparison, Malhotra (2019) compares machine learning models used in SDP. Similarly, Zhou et al. (2020) propose a hybrid SDP framework that combines different machine learning techniques. Finally, Shin et al. (2020) consider software complexity metrics as SDP inputs.
In recent years, deep neural network (Li et al., 2021), graph (Zhang et al., 2022), and ensemble-based (Kumar et al., 2023; Ahmed et al., 2024) models are becoming increasingly common in SDP research. All of them try to solve complex SDP problems by increasing computational and mathematical complexity. Nevertheless, current research suffers from multiple problems including computational complexity, lack of interpretability, inability to consider software evolution information, etc. Thus, despite recent advances in SDP research, there is still room for new ideas.
\subsubsection{Drawbacks of Existing Approaches}

Despite significant advancements, existing software defect prediction models suffer from several limitations:

\begin{itemize}
\item Heavy reliance on static code metrics, ignoring contextual and evolutionary information
\item Inability to capture complex feature interactions effectively
\item Sensitivity to class imbalance in datasets
\item High computational cost in deep learning and graph-based models
\item Requirement of large datasets for training advanced models
\item Increased model complexity in ensemble and hybrid approaches
\item Lack of interpretability in deep learning and black-box models
\item Extensive hyperparameter tuning requirements
\item Limited generalization across different datasets
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Proposed Solution to Overcome Limitations}

The proposed Quantum-Enhanced Explainable Context-Aware Software Defect Prediction Framework addresses the above limitations through the following contributions:

\begin{itemize}
\item Incorporation of both static and context-aware features to capture structural and evolutionary characteristics
\item Introduction of Quantum Feature Fusion (QFF) to model complex feature interactions and enhance representation
\item Application of SMOTE to effectively handle class imbalance
\item Use of XGBoost for efficient and scalable learning with high accuracy
\item Reduced computational complexity compared to deep learning and graph-based approaches
\item Integration of SHAP-based explainability to provide transparent and interpretable predictions
\item Minimal hyperparameter tuning with robust performance
\item Improved generalization across multiple datasets
\end{itemize}

\section{Methodology}

The proposed Quantum Enhanced Explainable Software Defect Prediction Framework consists of several stages including data preprocessing, quantum feature transformation, model training, and explainability analysis.

\subsection{Problem Statement}
Contemporary software projects are growing in terms of size, complexity, and dynamics because of continuous updates, joint development, and fast release schedules. Traditional approaches to software quality assurance based on exhaustive testing are inefficient and too expensive to implement successfully. Thus, intelligent mechanisms that will be able to detect defective software modules in the early stages of Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC) are needed.
Current software defect prediction models use static code metrics and conventional machine learning algorithms to classify modules as defective and non-defective. Although some models provide acceptable results in terms of accuracy, they still possess multiple shortcomings. First, these approaches cannot consider context-aware information obtained from software evolution because it has been found that software evolution and developer activities play an important role in software bugs occurrence. Second, as the number of used features increases, models face additional challenges including feature correlation and low class separability. Third, most models producing accurate predictions are highly sophisticated and difficult to interpret, which hinders their deployment in industrial settings. As such, the main problem addressed by this paper can be formulated as follows:
How to develop a software defect prediction framework capable of using context-aware features, enhancing feature representation, and generating explainable predictions without reducing computational efficiency?
To solve the aforementioned problem, the paper suggests developing Quantum-Enhanced Explainable Context-Aware Software Defect Prediction Framework that utilizes static and contextual features, Quantum Feature Fusion, XGBoost classifiers, and SHAP explainability layer.

The described algorithm consists of the following steps. First, software defect dataset is loaded, and both static and context-aware features are extracted from it. Static features include software module properties, whereas context-aware features are based on software evolution information.
Afterward, data preprocessing is required. Missing values in data are filled using the median value imputation. Features are normalized using Min-Max scaling algorithm. SMOTE sampling is implemented to deal with class imbalance.
Then, static and context-aware features are merged in order to improve model representation.
Subsequently, Quantum Feature Fusion is conducted using Fourier transformations to create high-dimensional representation of input features.
Finally, XGBoost model is trained using transformed features, and SHAP-based analysis is conducted to determine feature importance.

\subsection{Proposed Quantum-Enhanced Explainable Context-Aware Software Defect Prediction Framework}

This section presents the design and working of the proposed Quantum-Enhanced Explainable Context-Aware Software Defect Prediction Framework. The framework is designed to accurately identify defect-prone software modules by integrating static code metrics, change-history-based contextual features, quantum-inspired feature transformation, and explainable machine learning techniques.

\subsubsection{Proposed Algorithm}

The overall methodology of the proposed Quantum-Enhanced Explainable Context-Aware Software Defect Prediction Framework is summarized in Algorithm 1.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Quantum-Enhanced Software Defect Prediction Framework}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Software defect dataset $D$
\ENSURE Predicted defect labels and feature importance

\STATE Load dataset $D$
\STATE Extract static code metrics and context-aware features

\STATE \textbf{Data Preprocessing}
\STATE Handle missing values using median imputation
\STATE Normalize features using Min-Max scaling
\STATE Apply SMOTE to handle class imbalance

\STATE \textbf{Feature Integration}
\STATE Combine static and contextual features into feature matrix $X$

\STATE \textbf{Quantum Feature Fusion (QFF)}
\STATE Apply Fourier Transform: $X_q = |FFT(X)|$

\STATE \textbf{Model Training}
\STATE Train models: SVM, Random Forest, XGBoost

\STATE \textbf{Prediction}
\STATE Predict defect labels $\hat{Y}$ using trained models

\STATE \textbf{Explainability}
\STATE Compute SHAP values for feature importance

\RETURN Predicted labels $\hat{Y}$ and SHAP explanations

\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsubsection{Algorithm Explanation}

In the QFF phase, by performing a Fourier transform on the feature space of the combined feature matrix so that features communicate with each other and improve the class separability through projecting the features onto a high-dimensional space. Static code metrics represents internal structure of the modules while contextual features represents the software's evolution (e.g. Churn rate, number of commits).
Data preprocessing: Missing values are filled using median imputation and feature normalization is done using Min-Max scaling to transform each feature to have similar value range. The problem of class imbalance is tackled using SMOTE technique to oversample the minority class.
The extracted static and contextual features are then combined into a single feature matrix that encompasses both static and evolutionary aspect of software development.
The QFF stage takes the united feature matrix and employs a Fourier-based transformation on the feature space to make them interact with each other effectively and increase the class separability by projecting the feature space to a higher-dimensional space.
The projected feature space is used for training multiple ML models, namely Support Vector Machine(SVM), Random Forest (RF), and XGBoost. Out of them, XGBoost is taken as the main model for the sake of performance.
After training the models, it used to predict the defect prone modules. In order to get feature importance of features SHAP is used to evaluate how the model is predicting.

\subsubsection{Quantum Feature Extraction}

\text{7.1 Overview and Motivation}

Classical software metrics — such as Lines of Code (LOC), Coupling Between Objects (CBO), and Response for a Class (RFC) — reside in a low-dimensional Euclidean space where inter-feature relationships are often linear and insufficiently expressive. Quantum Feature Extraction (QFE) borrows the mathematical machinery of quantum mechanics to embed these classical vectors into a higher-dimensional, complex-valued Hilbert space. Within this enriched representation, non-linear dependencies become linearly separable, improving the downstream classifier's ability to distinguish defective from non-defective modules.

\text{7.2 Input Feature Vector}

Let $X \in \mathbb{R}^n$ denote the classical feature vector for a single software module composed of $n$ software metrics ($n = 21$ for NASA MDP datasets):

\begin{equation}
X = [x_1, x_2, x_3, \dots, x_n]^T, \quad x_k \in [0,1]
\tag{F1}
\end{equation}

where each component is normalized using Min-Max scaling:

\begin{equation}
x_k' = \frac{x_k - x_{k,\min}}{x_{k,\max} - x_{k,\min}}
\tag{F2}
\end{equation}

\text{7.3 Phase Encoding — Classical to Quantum State}

\begin{equation}
\phi_k = 2\pi \cdot x_k' \quad \Rightarrow \quad \psi_k = e^{i\phi_k} = \cos(\phi_k) + i \sin(\phi_k)
\tag{F3}
\end{equation}

\text{7.4 Quantum State Superposition}

\begin{equation}
|\psi\rangle = \bigotimes_{k=1}^{n} |\psi_k\rangle = \sum_{j=0}^{2^n - 1} \alpha_j |j\rangle
\tag{F4}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\sum_j |\alpha_j|^2 = 1
\tag{F5}
\end{equation}

\text{7.5 Quantum Fourier Transform (QFT)}

\begin{equation}
QFT|\psi\rangle =
\frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} \sum_{k=0}^{N-1}
\left( \sum_{j=0}^{N-1} \alpha_j \omega^{jk} \right) |k\rangle,
\quad \omega = e^{\frac{2\pi i}{N}}
\tag{F6}
\end{equation}

Practical implementation using FFT:

\begin{equation}
Q(X) = |\text{FFT}(X)|
\tag{F7}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
X_{QFE} =
\left[
|\text{FFT}(X)|,\;
\text{Re}\{\text{FFT}(X)\},\;
\text{Im}\{\text{FFT}(X)\},\;
\angle\{\text{FFT}(X)\}
\right]
\tag{F8}
\end{equation}

\text{7.6 Measurement and Feature Readout}

\begin{equation}
p_k = \frac{|X_k|^2}{\sum_j |X_j|^2}
\tag{F9}
\end{equation}

The top-K dominant frequency components are selected to form the compact feature representation.

\text{7.7 Summary of Quantum Feature Extraction}

\begin{itemize}
\item F1: $X = [x_1,...,x_n]^T$ — Classical input feature vector
\item F2: Min-Max normalization
\item F3: Phase encoding to complex plane
\item F4: Quantum superposition state
\item F5: Probability normalization
\item F6: Quantum Fourier Transform
\item F7: FFT-based transformation
\item F8: Quantum extracted feature set
\item F9: Measurement probability
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Performance Evaluation Metrics}

\textbf{Accuracy}

\begin{equation}
\text{Accuracy} = \frac{TP + TN}{TP + TN + FP + FN}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Precision}

\begin{equation}
\text{Precision} = \frac{TP}{TP + FP}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Recall}

\begin{equation}
\text{Recall} = \frac{TP}{TP + FN}
\end{equation}

\textbf{F1 Score}

\begin{equation}
F1 = \frac{2 \times (\text{Precision} \times \text{Recall})}{\text{Precision} + \text{Recall}}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC)}

\begin{equation}
\text{MCC} = \frac{TP \times TN - FP \times FN}
{\sqrt{(TP+FP)(TP+FN)(TN+FP)(TN+FN)}}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Quantum Feature Fusion}

\text{11.1 Problem Formulation}

We address the software defect binary classification problem: given a software module $M$ described by feature vector $X$, predict its defect label $Y \in \{0, 1\}$. Formally:

\begin{equation}
f : X \in \mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow Y \in \{0,1\}
\tag{Q1}
\end{equation}

The proposed QFF framework constructs an enriched feature space $\mathcal{H}_q \subset \mathbb{C}^m$ ($m \gg n$) using mapping $\Phi$.

\text{11.2 Context-Aware Feature Integration}

\begin{equation}
X_{\text{integrated}} = [SC \,||\, CH] = [x_1,...,x_s, c_1,...,c_h]^T \in \mathbb{R}^n
\tag{Q2}
\end{equation}

\text{11.3 SMOTE-Based Class Imbalance Correction}

\begin{equation}
x_{\text{new}} = x_i + \lambda (x_j - x_i), \quad \lambda \sim U(0,1)
\tag{Q3}
\end{equation}

\text{11.4 Quantum Feature Fusion — Unitary Transformation}

\begin{equation}
U = H \otimes R_z(\theta) \otimes C_{ent}
\tag{Q4}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
H = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 1 \\
1 & -1
\end{bmatrix} \otimes I_n
\tag{Q5}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
R_z(\theta_k) =
\begin{bmatrix}
e^{-i\theta_k/2} & 0 \\
0 & e^{i\theta_k/2}
\end{bmatrix}, \quad \theta_k = \pi x_k'
\tag{Q6}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
C_{ent}(\psi_i, \psi_j) = \psi_i \cdot \psi_j^* = e^{i(\phi_i - \phi_j)}
\tag{Q7}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
Q(X) = |\text{FFT}(U \cdot \psi(X))|
\tag{Q8}
\end{equation}

\section{Experimental Results and Performance Analysis}

This section presents the experimental evaluation and performance analysis of the proposed Quantum Enhanced Explainable Context Aware Software Defect Prediction Framework. The objective of the experiments is to assess the effectiveness of quantum inspired feature transformation combined with context aware metrics and an XGBoost classifier, as well as to evaluate the interpretability provided by SHAP based explainability.

\subsection{Implementation Details}

This section describes the implementation of the proposed Quantum-Enhanced Explainable Context-Aware Software Defect Prediction Framework, including the tools, algorithms, and execution flow.

\subsubsection{Implementation Environment}

The framework is implemented using standard data science tools.

\textbf{Hardware Configuration}
\begin{itemize}
\item Processor: Intel i5 / i7 or equivalent
\item RAM: Minimum 8 GB
\item Storage: 256 GB SSD or higher
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Software Environment}
\begin{itemize}
\item Operating System: Windows / Linux
\item Programming Language: Python 3.x
\item Development Tools: Jupyter Notebook / VS Code
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Libraries and Frameworks}
\begin{itemize}
\item NumPy – numerical computations
\item Pandas – data processing
\item Scikit-learn – preprocessing and evaluation
\item XGBoost – classification
\item SHAP – explainability
\item Qiskit / Quantum-inspired simulation – feature transformation
\item Matplotlib / Seaborn – visualization
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Feature Extraction Implementation}

\textbf{1) Static Code Metrics}
\begin{itemize}
\item LOC (Lines of Code)
\item WMC (Weighted Methods per Class)
\item CBO (Coupling Between Objects)
\item RFC (Response for a Class)
\item DIT (Depth of Inheritance Tree)
\item NOC (Number of Children)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{2) Change-History Metrics}
\begin{itemize}
\item Number of commits
\item Code churn
\item Number of developers
\item Change frequency
\end{itemize}

Static and contextual features are merged into a unified feature vector.

\subsubsection{Data Preprocessing Implementation}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Missing Value Handling:} Mean/median imputation
\item \textbf{Normalization:} Min-Max scaling
\item \textbf{Label Encoding:}
\begin{itemize}
\item Defective $\rightarrow 1$
\item Non-defective $\rightarrow 0$
\end{itemize}
\item \textbf{Train-Test Split:} 70\% training, 30\% testing
\end{itemize}





\subsubsection{XGBoost Classifier Implementation}

The transformed features are classified using XGBoost.

\textbf{Advantages:}
\begin{itemize}
\item High accuracy
\item Robust to noise
\item Handles high-dimensional data
\item Efficient training
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Key Parameters:}
\begin{itemize}
\item Learning rate
\item Max depth
\item Number of trees
\item Objective: Binary classification
\end{itemize}


\subsection{Experimental Setup}

All experiments were performed using Python programming language. Machine learning libraries were used for training and analyzing data in the context of defect prediction. In addition, SHAP package was employed for explainability analysis. Software defect datasets taken from PROMISE and NASA MDP repositories were used.

First, datasets were divided into training and testing parts. For each dataset, a 70:30 proportion was used. After that, feature normalization was conducted on both sets. As for data transformation, Quantum Feature Fusion was implemented only to training set while testing was conducted using the same transformation on a test set.


\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
The performance of defect predictors was analyzed using a number of widely accepted metrics obtained from the confusion matrix.


\begin{itemize}

\item \textbf{Precision:} Correctly predicted defective modules
\item \textbf{Recall:} Ability to detect defective modules
\item \textbf{F1 Score:} Balance between precision and recall
\item \textbf{FPR:} False Positive Rate
\item \textbf{FNR:} False Negative Rate
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Proposed Model Description}

In this section, we consider two versions of our proposed framework using two different models. These models include XGB Classical and XGB Quantum.

\subsubsection{XGB Classical}
In XGB Classical, original feature matrix consisting of static and contextual features is used to train a single model. XGB Classic works well with high-dimensional data and shows robust behavior while making predictions. However, the model performance is affected by feature representation and quality.

\subsubsection{XGB Quantum}
In contrast to XGB Classical model, XGB Quantum utilizes QFF to transform static and change history metrics before training. QFF helps transform static data to quantum-inspired representations. Such approach increases separation between classes and leads to better performance.
Together, these two variants demonstrate the effectiveness of quantum-inspired feature engineering in improving software defect prediction performance.



\subsection{Performance With QFF}

When quantum-inspired transformations were added to XGB models, performance was significantly improved. Quantum Feature Fusion helped to increase feature interactions and improve model separability.

\begin{itemize}
\item Improved accuracy and recall
\item Reduced false negative rate
\item Better robustness to noisy features
\end{itemize}

Thus, QFF helps improve the performance of machine learning models.



\subsection{Comparative Performance Evaluation}

Two models were compared:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Classical XGBoost (without QFF)
\item Quantum-Enhanced XGBoost (with QFF)
\end{enumerate}

Across all datasets, the Quantum Enhanced model consistently outperformed the baseline model. The performance gains were more pronounced in datasets that included both static and change history metrics, validating the effectiveness of context aware feature integration.

This shows that the proposed framework not only improves prediction accuracy, but also generalizes well across different software projects.

\subsection{Discussion}

The experimental results confirm that combining context aware metrics, quantum inspired feature transformation, and XGBoost classification results in a powerful and interpretable defect prediction system. Unlike traditional black box models, the proposed framework provides meaningful explanations without sacrificing accuracy.

The reduction in false negatives is particularly important in real world software engineering environments, where undetected defects can lead to increased maintenance costs and system failures. The integration of explainability further enhances trust and usability in industrial settings.

\section{Conclusion}
This paper proposes a Quantum Enhanced Explainable Context Aware Software Defect Prediction Framework for more accurate, understandable and practical use of defect prediction models. While conventional methods only exploit static software measures, this study combines static code measures with change history-based context aware features to better characterize software quality aspects.
A crucial part of the paper is the Quantum Feature Fusion (QFF) scheme based on quantum inspired transformation which can improve feature interactions and better separate defective from non-defective software modules. The proposed method achieves a better feature representation by embedding static software metrics into a higher dimensional quantum inspired feature space so that complex relationships often ignored by conventional feature engineering techniques are considered.
An XGBoost model, chosen for its efficiency, robustness, scalability and suitability for high dimensional data is used to classify the transformed features. Meanwhile, explainable artificial intelligence (XAI) techniques based on SHAP is added into the frame work in order to overcome the explainability limitation for most machine learning models. The explainable artificial intelligence (XAI) helps development and QA team to easily understand the contributions of individual features on prediction result and build trust.
Experimental results on several public software defect datasets verify that the proposed method can make more accurate prediction while provides effective explainability. It is demonstrated that the performance of defect prediction model can be significantly improved through incorporating context aware features and using quantum inspired feature fusion, coupled with explainable model.
This work provides a feasible solution to accurate, explainable and practical software defect prediction.

\section{Future Work}
Although the proposed framework demonstrates promising results, several directions for future research remain open. First, more advanced quantum inspired feature encoding techniques and hybrid quantum–classical models can be explored to further enhance feature representation capabilities. Second, the framework can be extended to include deep learning based contextual features, such as embeddings derived from source code or commit messages.
Future studies may also investigate cross project defect prediction to evaluate the generalizability of the proposed model across different software domains. Additionally, integrating online learning mechanisms could enable the model to adapt dynamically to evolving software systems.
